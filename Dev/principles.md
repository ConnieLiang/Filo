# Filo Design Principles

These principles govern every design and engineering decision in Filo. When in doubt, refer back here.

---

## Core Belief

> Email overload is not a volume problem. It's a decision fatigue problem.

Filo helps people understand, act on, and move past email without wasting attention.

---

## Principles

### 1. Reduce Cognitive Friction

Every feature must reduce thinking, not shift it elsewhere.

- If a screen requires the user to make a decision that Filo could make, the design is wrong.
- Defaults should be smart enough that most users never change them.
- Scanning > reading. Users should grasp meaning at a glance.
- Elements that behave differently must look different. External links and in-app navigation cannot share the same visual affordance in a list — users must be able to predict what happens before they tap.

### 2. AI-First, Not AI-Loud

Filo is an AI email client — that should be clear from the first interaction. But the AI earns trust by being useful, not by showing off.

- Make AI visible where it creates value: summaries, smart labels, instant write, and task extraction should be front and center — not hidden behind menus.
- Let the AI speak for itself through results, not branding. Users should think "this is smart," not "this is using AI."
- No chatbot interfaces, no forced prompts, no "talk to your inbox" gimmicks. AI is woven into the product, not bolted on top.
- When AI acts, show what it did and let users correct it. Confidence without arrogance.

### 3. Opinionated Defaults

Filo makes decisions so users don't have to.

- Power users can go deeper, but only if they choose to.
- The default experience is the primary experience; it must be excellent on its own.
- Preference panels and toggles are a last resort, not a first response to uncertainty.

### 4. Respect Attention

No dopamine-driven UI. No fake urgency. No dark patterns.

- Notification copy must be honest about importance.
- Animation and motion only when it clarifies meaning — never for delight alone.
- Badge counts, red dots, and interrupting modals need strong justification.

---

## The Litmus Test

If a feature:

- Adds options → it needs to justify itself.
- Adds decisions → it needs to justify itself.
- Adds visual weight → it needs to justify itself.
- Adds explanation → it needs to justify itself.
- Adds a new capability → validate the value before discussing implementation.

**Less is the feature.**

---

## Visual Direction

- Clean, modern UI
- Soft colors, controlled contrast
- Minimal ornamentation
- Motion only when it clarifies meaning
- No visual noise disguised as delight

The interface should feel: **stable, predictable, trustworthy, lightweight.**

---

## Copy & Voice

- Short sentences
- Plain language
- No hype words
- Name AI capabilities plainly — say what they do, not how magical they are
- UI labels (tabs, group headers, buttons) should be single words or the shortest possible phrase. No descriptive suffixes. "AI" not "AI Features". "Mail" not "Mail Settings".

Good copy answers:

1. What is happening?
2. Why does it matter?
3. What should I do next?

The tone is grounded, human, slightly self-aware — like a thoughtful person, not a product.

---

## Brand Personality

Filo is: calm, clear, thoughtful, slightly opinionated, never loud.

> A smart, composed assistant who knows when to speak and when to stay quiet.
